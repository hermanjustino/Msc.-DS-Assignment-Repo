Pedaling Through Policy: A Data-Driven Look at Toronto’s Bike Lane Debate

In recent years, urban infrastructure planning has become a focal point of debates surrounding sustainable city living. One prominent discussion in Toronto is the provincial government’s proposal to remove bike lanes along Yonge, Bloor, and University Streets. These streets are central to the city's downtown core and serve as vital thoroughfares for commuters, cyclists, and pedestrians alike. The removal of bike lanes raises questions about its potential impact on the neighborhoods these streets traverse, including changes to commute patterns, accessibility, and the quality of life for residents. This article explores the characteristics of the neighborhoods directly affected by the proposed changes using data from the 2021 Census, sourced from the City of Toronto’s open data portal. By analyzing demographics, commute patterns, and socio-economic factors, this analysis aims to provide a data-driven perspective on the implications of this decision.

Task Overview The primary objective of this project is to create a visualization-rich, interactive article to assess the impact of bike lane removal on affected neighborhoods. The article uses data visualization techniques to: Explore Demographics: Highlight age, income, education, and work industry distributions across neighborhoods along Yonge, Bloor, and University Streets. Analyze Commute Patterns: Examine how residents of these neighborhoods travel, including the proportion of cycling commuters and the average commute times. Profile Neighborhoods: Compare these neighborhoods to city-wide trends, emphasizing their unique attributes and potential vulnerabilities to bike lane removal. The visualizations serve to make complex data accessible to a broader audience, including city planners, policymakers, and residents interested in understanding the local impact of this controversial policy proposal.
